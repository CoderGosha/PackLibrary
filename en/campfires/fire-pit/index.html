Fire pit (Dakota fire hole)

Essentially a stove dug into the ground with a fire burning inside it, also known as a Dakota fire hole or Polynesian fire pit. Very convenient for cooking, almost invisible from a distance, and doesn't radiate heat outward, so you can dry gear right next to it without risking scorching it. The main downside is it gives almost no light, and you can't dig one without a shovel.

  1. Pick a spot on slightly raised ground and dig a hole for the fire itself, sized so the fire fits entirely inside it.
  2. Dig a second, narrower tunnel into it for airflow — its diameter should be at least a third of the main hole's diameter.
  3. Light a fire inside the main hole using a normal lay (a teepee lay works well).
  4. When cooking, make sure your pot doesn't completely block the top opening — otherwise the draft is lost and the fire starts to die out.
